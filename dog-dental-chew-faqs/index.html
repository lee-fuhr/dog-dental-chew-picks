dog dental chews questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Dog dental chews can help reduce plaque and tartar when used consistently, but they are not a replacement for brushing or professional cleanings. Choosing the right size, texture, and ingredients, and watching your dog during chewing, is key to safety and effectiveness.

How do dental chews actually clean teeth?

Most dental chews work through a combination of mechanical abrasion and chemical additives. The chewing action scrapes away soft plaque and massages the gums, while some chews contain enzymes or antiseptic ingredients (like chlorhexidine or zinc salts) that help reduce bacteria. The texture matters: firm, porous textures tend to scrub better than hard, brittle ones, which may break into sharp pieces. That said, no chew reaches every tooth surface. The effectiveness varies by brand, shape, and how your dog chews. The Veterinary Oral Health Council (VOHC) awards a seal to products that meet evidence-based plaque and tartar reduction targets. Look for that seal if you want a proven choice.

Are dental chews safe for all dogs?

Safety depends on your dog’s size, chewing style, and any existing health issues. For aggressive chewers, very hard chews, such as some antlers, bones, or compressed rawhide, pose a risk of chipped teeth or intestinal blockages. Even softer dental chews should be supervised, especially if your dog tends to gulp large pieces without thorough chewing. Puppies and senior dogs with sensitive teeth may need softer treats. Dogs with pancreatitis or food allergies must avoid certain fats or protein sources. Always check the ingredient list and consult your vet if you’re unsure. No treat is 100% risk-free, but the right fit makes it very low risk for most dogs.

How do I pick the right size and texture for my dog?

Size is straightforward: the chew should be large enough that your dog cannot swallow it whole. Most brands have size charts based on weight, follow those. A chew that is too small is a choking hazard; one that is too big may not be used at all. For texture, aim for something that yields slightly under pressure but does not splinter. Many owners prefer chews that soften as they are chewed (like sweet potato chews or certain starch-based dental sticks) over hard nylon or antler. The best texture also depends on your dog’s chewing style. A gentle chewer may need less durability; a power chewer needs something tougher that still passes safety tests. Read owner reviews and look for VOHC-accepted options to narrow the field.

How often should I give my dog a dental chew?

Most veterinarians and manufacturers recommend one dental chew per day, but that can vary based on your dog’s dietary needs and the treat’s calorie content. Overdoing it can lead to weight gain or digestive upset. If the chew is high in calories, you may need to reduce your dog’s regular meal portion. Consistency matters more than frequency. Giving a daily chew helps maintain a routine that prevents plaque buildup. However, if your dog has a medical condition like diabetes or obesity, discuss frequency with your vet. And never give multiple chews in one day unless the product instructions explicitly allow it.

Do dental chews replace brushing or professional cleanings?

No, and it’s important to be honest about that. Even the best dental chews reduce plaque and tartar by a certain percentage, but they cannot remove established calculus below the gumline. Brushing your dog’s teeth with a pet-safe toothpaste is far more effective at keeping gums healthy. Dental chews are best thought of as a helpful supplement, not a substitute. Professional cleanings under anesthesia are still necessary for most dogs every year or two, especially if they have heavy tartar or signs of periodontal disease. A good home care routine, including chews, can stretch the time between cleanings but won’t eliminate them entirely.

What ingredients should I look for, or avoid?

Look for simple, identifiable ingredients. For dental benefits, enzymes like glucose oxidase or lactoperoxidase can inhibit bacteria. Some chews include green tea extract, vitamin C, or mint for breath freshening. The VOHC seal is a stronger indicator of efficacy than any single ingredient, though. Avoid chews with excessive artificial colors, preservatives (like BHA, BHT, ethoxyquin), or high sugar content. Corn, wheat, and soy are common fillers that offer no nutritional or dental value. Dogs with allergies may need limited-ingredient chews. Also steer clear of chews that are extremely hard, even if labeled “natural,” toughness can lead to tooth fractures.

Frequently asked questions

Can puppies use dental chews?

Yes, but choose soft, gentle chews specifically sized for puppies. Puppies have sensitive mouths and baby teeth that are more prone to damage. Wait until the puppy is at least 12 weeks old and consult your vet for the best age to start.

What if my dog swallows a chew whole?

Stop giving that size or type immediately. Swallowing a chew whole is a choking and blockage risk. Switch to a larger size or a different texture that forces more chewing. Always supervise your dog with any dental chew.

Are grain-free dental chews better?

Not necessarily for dental benefit. Grain-free matters only if your dog has a grain allergy or intolerance. The dental efficacy depends on texture and enzymes, not the presence or absence of grains. Focus on the VOHC seal and your dog’s tolerance.

How long should a dental chew last my dog?

It varies by dog and chew type, but a good rule is that the chew should last at least a few minutes of active chewing. If it’s gone in under 30 seconds, it’s probably too small or too soft. If it lasts more than 20 minutes daily, it may be too hard. Monitor and adjust.

Do VOHC-approved chews really work better?

The VOHC seal means the product has passed standardized trials showing significant plaque and tartar reduction. It’s the most reliable evidence you can get without doing your own study. Not all effective chews carry the seal, but it’s a strong indicator.

Can dental chews cause upset stomach or diarrhea?

Yes, especially if your dog isn’t used to them or if the chew contains rich ingredients like hydrolyzed protein or added fats. Introduce any new chew gradually, half a chew for a few days, and watch for loose stools. Discontinue if symptoms persist.

In shortA well-chosen dental chew can be a safe, enjoyable part of your dog’s oral care routine, but it works best as one tool among several. Pair it with regular brushing, the right diet, and veterinary checkups. When you pick a chew that fits your dog’s size, chewing style, and health needs, you’ll get the most benefit with the least risk. Always read labels, supervise chew sessions, and ask your vet if you’re ever in doubt.
